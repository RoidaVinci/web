Uncountable Set with No Middle Points

Consider the problem of finding an uncountable set within \( \mathbb{R} \) that contains no middle points. In other words, find a set \( X \subset \mathbb{R} \) such that for any two distinct points \( x, y \in X \), the point \( \frac{x+y}{2} \) does not belong to \( X \), and yet \( X \) is uncountable.

Show Solution
Solution:

Consider the set \( X := \left\lbrace \sum_{k \in U} 2^{-k} : U \subset 2\mathbb{N} - 1 \right\rbrace \).

Prove Uncountability and No Middle Points
Proof:

Uncountability:

We will prove that \( X \) is uncountable by providing a one to one map between \( X \) and the interval \( [0,1) \).

We efine the function:

$$ \kappa: U \in \mathcal{P}(2\mathbb{N} - 1) \longmapsto \kappa(U) = \left\{\dfrac{n+1}{2}: n \in U \right\} \in \mathcal{P}(\mathbb{N}) $$

\( \kappa \) is an one to one map between \( \mathcal{P}(2\mathbb{N} - 1) \) and \( \mathcal{P}(\mathbb{N}) \). Therefore, the composition:

$$ \xi : x = \sum_{k \in U} 2^{-k} \in X \longmapsto \xi(x) = \sum_{k \in \kappa(U)} 2^{-k} \in [0,1) $$

is a one to one map, showing that \( X \) is uncountable. More clearly, this function maps every real number in the unit interval to its shortest binary expansion, which is one to one as every number has an unique shortest binary representation. This is mapped to the number that results from setting even positions of the binary expansion to zero, which is one to one because of \( \kappa \) being one to one.

No Middle Points:

Let \( x, y \in X \) and \( x \neq y \). Without loss of generality, assume \( x \) has a 1 in an odd position where \( y \) has a 0. When summing them in base 2, that 1 remains in the same odd position, and when divided by two, it moves to the next even position. Thus, \( \frac{x+y}{2} \not\in X \).
